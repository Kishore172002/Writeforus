Getting louver spacing right for a Chennai balcony

2026-06-10 · 6 min read

We tracked airflow and rain intrusion across three balcony louver installs through this year's monsoon — one at 15mm gaps, one at 25mm, and one on an adjustable frame. The results were less about a single "correct" spacing and more about which side of the building you're on.

What we measured

On a north-facing balcony shielded from driving rain, the 25mm gap moved noticeably more air and kept the space usable through humid afternoons. The same spacing on a south-facing balcony let in visible spray during the July storms — water reached almost a metre in from the louver line.

A rough rule that held up

  • Sheltered or upper-floor balconies with limited driving rain: 20–25mm gaps for better airflow
  • Exposed balconies facing prevailing monsoon wind: 12–15mm gaps, or an adjustable louver frame
  • Anywhere storing electronics or fabric nearby: adjustable is worth the extra cost, full stop

The adjustable option

The adjustable-frame install cost roughly 30% more than a fixed 20mm louver but let the owner close the slats tight during storms and open them fully otherwise — the only setup in our sample that didn't compromise on one side or the other.

What to ask your fabricator

Ask which direction the balcony faces relative to prevailing monsoon wind before agreeing on a gap, not after installation. Most fabricators default to a standard spacing unless you specifically raise this — it's rarely part of their default questionnaire.
